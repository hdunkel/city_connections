A graph of Germany built from street names

The Rule

You can travel from A to B
if city A has a street named "B-er Straße"

Example: Lüneburg → Greifswald via Greifswalder Straße

"City" means any of the 3,015 German municipalities (Gemeinden) listed in Wikidata. Streets are matched from OpenStreetMap.

The Network

Connected?

A weakly connected component groups cities you can reach by ignoring edge direction. A strongly connected component requires mutual reachability — a path exists in both directions between every pair of cities in the group.

Islands

Cities in different weakly connected components share no street-name path in either direction. Of 3,015 municipalities, 21 form isolated islands outside the main network.

The Longest Road

The diameter is the longest of all shortest paths in the main connected component. It tells you the worst-case number of hops between any two reachable cities.

Hubs

Betweenness centrality (0–1) measures how often a city lies on the shortest path between two other cities. A high score means the city is a critical transit point — removing it would disconnect many routes.

Most Referenced

In-degree: how many other German cities have a street named after this city (e.g. "Berliner Straße"). A high in-degree means the city is culturally prominent across Germany.

Most Connected

Out-degree: how many different cities this city has streets named after. Cities with high out-degree tend to be suburban or near major hubs, with many streets named after neighbouring towns.
